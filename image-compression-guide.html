Image Compression for the Web: A Practical Guide

By ToolHub Editorial · June 27, 2026 · 6 min read

Heavy images are the #1 cause of slow websites. Learn the difference between lossy and lossless compression, when to use each format, and how to shrink files without losing quality.

Why image weight matters

According to the HTTP Archive, images make up roughly 50% of the average web page's total payload. A 4 MB hero image on a 4G mobile connection takes 8+ seconds to download — long enough that 53% of users abandon the page. Compressing images is the single highest-ROI performance optimization you can make.

Lossy vs lossless

Lossy compression (JPEG, WebP at low quality) permanently discards information your eye can't easily perceive. You get massive size reductions — often 80%+ — at the cost of fine detail. Best for photographs.

Lossless compression (PNG, WebP at quality 100) preserves every pixel. Smaller savings but no quality loss. Best for screenshots, line art, logos and anything with sharp edges or text.

Format cheat sheet

  • JPEG — photos with smooth gradients. Universal browser support.
  • PNG — graphics with transparency, sharp edges, few colors. Lossless.
  • WebP — modern Google format. 25–35% smaller than JPEG at the same visual quality. Universal support since 2020.
  • AVIF — newest format. Even smaller than WebP. Support is now ~95% of browsers but encoding is slow.
  • SVG — vector graphics. Infinitely scalable, tiny file size for icons and logos.

Quality settings to use

  • Photos: quality 0.75–0.85 is almost always visually indistinguishable from the original
  • Background images: 0.6 is often enough; nobody studies them up close
  • Product photos that need detail: 0.85–0.9
  • Hero images: 0.8 plus modern format (WebP)

Beyond compression

  • Resize before compressing. A 4000×3000 photo doesn't need to be served at full size. Resize to the display size first.
  • Use srcset to serve different resolutions to different devices.
  • Lazy-load below-the-fold images with loading="lazy".
  • Specify dimensions to prevent layout shift.
