How to Create a Strong Password in 2026 (Complete Guide)

By ToolHub Editorial · June 27, 2026 · 6 min read

Strong passwords are your first defense against account compromise. Learn what makes a password truly secure, how attackers crack weak ones, and best practices to follow.

Why password strength is the #1 security control you own

Every year, billions of credentials leak in data breaches. The single biggest predictor of whether your accounts get hijacked isn't the brand of antivirus you run — it's whether you use long, unique, random passwords. This guide walks through exactly what makes a password strong, what attackers actually do, and the workflow that takes you from "I reuse one password everywhere" to "every account has its own 20-character random string."

How attackers crack passwords

Three techniques dominate:

  1. Credential stuffing. An attacker takes a username/password list leaked from one site and tries the same combination on 1,000 other sites. If you reuse passwords, every breach becomes your breach.
  2. Dictionary attacks. Software tries every word in the English language, plus common variations (password123, P@ssw0rd!). Modern wordlists contain billions of entries.
  3. Brute force. The attacker tries every combination of characters. Length is the only thing that protects you here — each extra character multiplies the time needed.

What length is enough?

  • Under 8 characters — cracked in seconds, no matter the complexity
  • 8–11 characters — minutes to hours on a single GPU
  • 12–15 characters — days to years, depending on character set
  • 16+ characters of random output — practically uncrackable with today's hardware

Length beats complexity

A 20-character lowercase-only password (vmqlxrkebpwhydcsoian) is mathematically stronger than an 8-character password with every character set (P@1!aZx#). Always favour length.

The three-rule workflow

  1. Unique. Every site gets its own password. No exceptions.
  2. Random. Generate with a tool — never invent passwords yourself. Human brains are predictable.
  3. Stored in a password manager. 1Password, Bitwarden, KeePass, even your browser's built-in manager. Anything beats reuse.

Two-factor authentication is not optional

Even the strongest password can be phished. Enable 2FA — preferably using an authenticator app or hardware key, not SMS — on every account that supports it.
