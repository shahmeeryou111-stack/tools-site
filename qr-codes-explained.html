QR Codes Explained: How They Work and When to Use Them

By ToolHub Editorial · June 27, 2026 · 6 min read

QR codes have become a universal way to share links, contact info and Wi-Fi credentials. Here is how they work under the hood and best practices for creating them.

What a QR code actually is

A QR (Quick Response) code is a 2D barcode invented in 1994 by Denso Wave for tracking automotive parts. Unlike traditional 1D barcodes that hold ~20 characters, a QR code can encode up to 4,296 alphanumeric characters in the same physical space — by using a grid instead of a line.

The anatomy of a QR code

  • Position markers — the three large squares in the corners that tell scanners orientation
  • Timing patterns — alternating black/white lines that help with scaling
  • Data area — the actual encoded payload
  • Error correction — Reed-Solomon redundancy that lets the code be read even if up to 30% is damaged or obscured

What you can encode

  • URLs (most common use)
  • Plain text
  • vCard contact info
  • Wi-Fi credentials (network name, password, encryption type)
  • Calendar events
  • SMS or email templates
  • Geo coordinates

Best practices for QR codes that actually scan

  1. Print at 2×2 cm minimum. Smaller and most phones struggle.
  2. Quiet zone. Leave at least 4 modules (cells) of white space around the code.
  3. High contrast. Dark code on a light background — not the other way around if you can help it.
  4. Pick the right error-correction level. Higher levels survive damage but make the code denser. Use Q (25%) for outdoor signage; L (7%) for screen display.
  5. Test on multiple phones before printing 10,000 flyers.

Static vs dynamic QR codes

Static codes embed the destination directly — change the URL and you must reprint. Dynamic codes link to a short redirect URL you control; you can update the destination later without changing the printed code. For marketing campaigns, always go dynamic.
